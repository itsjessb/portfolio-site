The 7-Agent Creative Ops Playbook

Seven AI agents that give marketing and creative teams their week back, and the process each one needs before it will work. Built from a transformation that delivered an 80% productivity increase by hand, before a single agent ran.

The premise

AI agents don't fix broken processes. They accelerate them.

Every one of these seven agents exists because I watched a talented creative team lose its week to coordination work: chasing missing brief details, playing air-traffic control with requests, writing status updates nobody read until they were out of date, and nagging executives for approvals that stalled projects silently.

At Blue Yonder, we fixed that without AI. We mapped every intake path, killed the redundant steps, standardized the briefs, defined service tiers, and deployed a system of record in 60 days. Team productivity rose 80%. Turnaround dropped 15%. On-time completion hit 94%.

This playbook is what I'd automate today, with the benefit of knowing exactly which coordination tasks were eating the team. I did them manually first. Each agent below maps to one of those tasks. And each comes with the part most AI content skips: the process precondition. Point an agent at a broken workflow and it doesn't fix anything. It just produces malformed work faster.

Rule of the playbook: if you can't run the step consistently as a checklist executed by a human, you are not ready to hand it to an agent.

Before you build anything: the readiness check

Score yourself honestly. Each "no" is a two-week process project that comes before the corresponding agent. Doing that project is where most of the productivity gain actually lives.

  • Work enters the team through one front door (a single form or queue), not email, chat, and hallway conversations.
  • A standardized brief exists, and everyone knows which fields are required before work starts.
  • Work types have defined service tiers with turnaround standards. A social cutdown is 3 days; a campaign concept is 3 weeks.
  • Projects move through named stages that mean the same thing on every project.
  • Every deliverable has a named approver, and a backup for when they're out.

Agent 01 · The front door

The Intake Checker

The pain: half of incoming requests are missing the audience, the dimensions, the due date, or the approver. Your designers spend their mornings playing detective instead of designing.

What it does

Watches the intake queue and validates every new request against your brief standard. Complete requests enter the queue marked ready. Incomplete ones get an immediate, friendly reply to the requester listing exactly what's missing and why it matters. The agent re-checks when the requester updates, and promotes the brief the moment it's whole.

Trigger logic
  1. New request lands in the intake form or queue.
  2. Agent parses the submission against required fields: objective, audience, deliverable type, specs and dimensions, due date, approver, campaign link.
  3. For free-text fields, an LLM pass judges substance. "Make it pop" is not an objective.
  4. Gaps found: reply to the requester within minutes, listing the missing items with a link to the brief guide and one example of a good answer.
  5. All fields present and substantive: mark ready, stamp the intake-complete time, hand off to the Router (Agent 02).
Build notes

Lightest lift of the seven. Wrike request forms plus a Wrike AI agent covers most of it natively. The platform-agnostic version is form to n8n or Make, Claude for the free-text judgment, reply via email or Slack. A day of build, a week of tuning the "substantive answer" prompt.

Metric to watch

Percentage of requests entering the queue complete. Measure the baseline first; it will be embarrassing, which is the point. Also watch rework loops per project. The equivalent manual fix at Blue Yonder was the single biggest contributor to the 80%.

Process precondition

One front door and one brief standard. If requests can still reach designers by DM, the agent is a suggestion, not a system. Close the side doors first. That's a leadership conversation, not a workflow build.

Rollout note: announce it as faster starts, never as gatekeeping. "Complete briefs skip triage and start same-day." Requesters adopt whatever gets their work done sooner. Grandfather everything in flight.

Agent 02 · The traffic controller

The Request Router

The pain: complete requests sit unassigned for days waiting on a triage meeting, and one coordinator carries the whole team's assignment logic in their head.

What it does

Takes every brief the Intake Checker marks ready, classifies it by work type and complexity tier, checks who has capacity and the right skills, and assigns it. The due window is set by the service tier, and both assignee and requester get a notification with the expected delivery date.

Trigger logic
  1. Brief flagged ready by Agent 01.
  2. Classify: deliverable type, service tier, complexity score.
  3. Query the skills and capacity map: current workload per person from the PM system, skill tags per work type.
  4. Assign to the best available match; back-plan the due date from the tier standard.
  5. Notify the assignee with the full brief and the requester with the owner and expected date. Log the routing decision for the weekly fairness review.
Build notes

n8n or Make pulling workload from the Wrike or Asana API; Claude handles classification against your tier definitions. The hard part isn't the automation. It's writing down the assignment logic your coordinator currently improvises.

Metric to watch

Time from ready to assigned. Target same business day, from a baseline that's often 2 to 4 days. Also watch workload balance across the team.

Process precondition

Service tiers with turnaround standards, and a skills and capacity map. If you can't articulate why a request should go to Sam and not Priya, neither can the agent.

Rollout note: run two weeks in shadow mode. The agent suggests, the coordinator confirms with one click. You'll catch the edge cases, and the coordinator becomes the agent's sponsor instead of its casualty. Their job upgrades from air-traffic control to exception handling and capacity planning.

Agent 03 · The early warning

The Deadline Risk Sentinel

The pain: most teams find out a project is late when it's late. By then the options are apologize or heroics.

What it does

Flags projects while they're still trending late. It watches task-level progress against the plan, checks whether upstream dependencies (usually approvals) have cleared, and pings the owner privately with the two specific things that would pull the project back on track.

Trigger logic
  1. Daily scan of every active project's stage, task velocity, and remaining runway.
  2. Risk score: percent of plan consumed versus percent of work complete, plus pending-approval age, plus assignee load.
  3. Score crosses the threshold: private message to the project owner with what's trending late, why (the two biggest drags), and the smallest actions that fix it.
  4. No action in 48 hours and risk still rising: offer the owner an escalation draft. The human decides to send it.
Build notes

Scheduled n8n workflow against the PM system's API. The risk heuristic starts as three simple rules, not a model. Wrike's native AI risk prediction covers a chunk of this if you're on Wrike; the agent's value-add is the private, actionable nudge.

Metric to watch

Share of at-risk projects flagged at least 3 days before the slip would land, and the on-time completion rate. 94% on-time is an achievable bar. I've seen it held for quarters.

Process precondition

Standardized project stages with realistic duration baselines. "Trending late" is only computable against a plan that means something. That's why this agent comes after 01 and 02, whose output keeps the data honest.

Rollout note: pings go to the owner only, never a public channel. A risk agent that shames people teaches them to game the statuses, and then it's worse than useless. It's confidently wrong.

Agent 04 · The Friday ghostwriter

The Campaign Status Reporter

The pain: the weekly status doc takes someone two hours to compile, is out of date by Monday, and still doesn't stop the "quick question, where's the launch banner?" pings.

What it does

Compiles the status update nobody has to write: what shipped, what's in flight with expected dates, what's blocked and on whom. Pulled straight from the PM system, summarized per audience (the exec version and the requester version are different documents), and drafted for a two-minute human review before sending.

Trigger logic
  1. Scheduled run, Friday 7:00 a.m., plus on-demand for launches.
  2. Query all active work: completions this week, stage changes, blocked items with the blocking party, next week's due list.
  3. LLM pass composes per-audience digests in the team's voice. Bullets for execs, detail for requesters.
  4. Draft lands with the ops lead for review; one click to send to each distribution.
Build notes

The friendliest first build after 01: cron to PM API to Claude to an email or Slack draft. Total build measured in hours. The craft is in the audience-splitting prompt. Write the exec digest to answer one question: "do I need to do anything?" Nothing else.

Metric to watch

Hours of status-writing eliminated (typically 2 to 4 per week per team) and the count of ad-hoc "where is X?" pings, which should visibly decay by week 4.

Process precondition

Statuses in the tool must be current. The report is only as honest as the board. Agents 01 through 03 are what keep that data real. Run this agent on a neglected board and you'll distribute fiction on schedule.

Rollout note: keep the human-review step for at least a month. The first time a digest is wrong in public, trust dies. The review step is cheap insurance while accuracy earns its reputation.

Agent 05 · The librarian

The Naming Convention Enforcer

The pain: "final_v3_FINAL(2).psd." Assets nobody can find get rebuilt from scratch, and the rebuild costs more than the original.

What it does

Watches wherever assets land (the DAM, shared drives, the PM system's attachments) and checks every new file against the naming convention. Unambiguous fixes are applied automatically. Ambiguous ones trigger a nudge to the uploader with the corrected name pre-filled, one click to accept. Keeps a searchable index as a byproduct.

Trigger logic
  1. New file event in watched locations.
  2. Parse the filename against the pattern: campaign_deliverable_size_version_date.
  3. Metadata sufficient to fix confidently: rename, log, done.
  4. Ambiguous: message the uploader with the suggested compliant name; accepting applies it.
  5. Weekly compliance digest to the ops lead with trends, repeat offenders (handled privately), and coverage.
Build notes

Storage webhooks (Drive, Dropbox, DAM) to n8n, rename via API. The LLM earns its keep on inference: reading a messy filename plus its folder context and proposing the compliant version.

Metric to watch

Percent of new assets compliant (expect 90%+ by week 6) and self-reported time-to-find in the monthly team pulse. The second number is the one leadership feels.

Process precondition

A documented, one-page naming convention the team actually agreed to. The agent enforces an agreement; it cannot create one. The workshop where the team writes that page is the real deliverable. The agent just makes it permanent.

Rollout note: suggestion with one-click accept beats silent auto-rename for the first month. People need to see the pattern before they trust the robot touching their files.

Agent 06 · The polite persistence machine

The Approval Follow-up Nudger

The pain: projects don't die loudly. They stall silently, waiting on a sign-off from someone whose inbox ate the request. And somebody on your team has become the professional nag.

What it does

Tracks every pending approval and runs a graduated follow-up sequence so a human never has to: a gentle nudge, then a nudge with the consequence date, then escalation to the named backup approver. Logs approval latency by stakeholder. That data turns "reviews feel slow" into a fixable fact.

Trigger logic
  1. Item enters awaiting approval; the clock starts.
  2. Day 2: friendly nudge. What's waiting, one line of context, direct link, a 30-second ask.
  3. Day 4: second nudge, now with the consequence. "Launch date moves to the 18th if this clears after Thursday."
  4. Day 5: escalate to the designated backup approver, original approver cc'd. Nobody is surprised, because the policy was agreed in advance.
  5. Approval lands: thank-you, clock stops, latency logged.
Build notes

PM-system approval states to an n8n scheduler to Slack or email. Trivial mechanically. The LLM's job is tone: nudges that read as helpful context, not automated nagging. Test the copy on your most senior stakeholder first.

Metric to watch

Mean approval latency (typical baseline 4 to 6 days; target under 2) and the share of projects stalled more than 5 days on sign-off, which should approach zero.

Process precondition

Named approvers, an agreed escalation path, and leadership's blessing on the escalation rule. The agent enforces policy; it must never be the one deciding it. Get the VP to announce the rule, then let the robot apply it evenly.

Rollout note: the fairness is the feature. Humans nag selectively and resent it. The agent nudges everyone identically, including the executives. That is exactly why it works, and exactly why you need the air cover first.

Agent 07 · The day-zero machine

The Project Kickoff Auto-Setup

The pain: every approved project begins with 45 minutes of ceremony. Create the workspace, copy the template, rename the tasks, build the folders, retype the brief into a kickoff doc. Done slightly differently every time.

What it does

The moment a brief is approved, it stands up the entire project: the right template for the work type, tasks back-planned from the due date, owners pre-assigned from the Router's decision, folder structure named per convention, a kickoff summary pre-filled from the brief, and calendar holds for the milestones. The team walks into a project that's already set up correctly.

Trigger logic
  1. Brief hits approved.
  2. Select the project template by work type and tier.
  3. Instantiate tasks; back-plan dates from the delivery date using tier standards.
  4. Create the folder tree with compliant names. Agent 05's convention, from birth.
  5. Generate the kickoff summary from the brief, drop it in the workspace, notify the team with links.
Build notes

Wrike blueprints or Asana templates triggered via API from n8n or Make; Claude writes the kickoff summary. Mostly configuration, not code. The effort is in building good templates, which is a process gift that keeps giving even without the agent.

Metric to watch

Setup time per project (45 minutes to about 2) and template coverage: the percent of new projects that fit an existing template, which tells you how standardized your work types really are.

Process precondition

Project templates per work type. Building them forces the conversation most teams have never had: what kinds of work do we actually do, and what does "set up right" mean for each? That inventory is worth more than the automation.

Rollout note: ship this one early even though it's numbered last. It's pure delight, no behavior change required from anyone, and it buys goodwill for the agents that do ask people to change.

Sequencing

The build order (and why)

Don't build seven agents at once. Build in waves that respect the dependency underneath everything: the later agents are only as smart as the data the earlier ones keep clean.

WaveAgentsWhy this order
Wave 1
Weeks 1–4		 01 Intake Checker
07 Kickoff Auto-Setup		 Fix the front door and the first day. One agent asks requesters to change behavior; the other gives the team a gift. Together they make every project born clean.
Wave 2
Weeks 5–8		 02 Request Router
04 Status Reporter		 With intake clean and projects standardized, routing logic has real data and the status digest tells the truth. This is the wave leadership notices.
Wave 3
Weeks 9–12		 03 Deadline Sentinel
06 Approval Nudger
05 Naming Enforcer		 The intelligence layer. Risk prediction and latency tracking only work on top of honest stages and current statuses, which waves 1 and 2 created.
The 30-day pilot version

If you can only do one thing this month: standardize the brief (week 1), close the side doors (week 2), ship the Intake Checker (week 3), measure complete-brief rate and time-to-start (week 4). One agent, one metric, one before and after. That's a pilot an executive will fund the rest from.

What good looks like at day 90

  • Requests enter complete or bounce back within minutes. No detective work.
  • Assignment happens the same day, by rule, not by meeting.
  • Friday status writes itself; leadership pings drop off.
  • At-risk projects surface days early, privately, with fixes attached.
  • Approvals clear in under two days, because the follow-up never sleeps and never plays favorites.
  • The team's coordination hours (typically 20 to 30% of a creative team's week) go back into the work you hired them for.
